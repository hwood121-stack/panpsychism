Russellian Monism

A view that physics describes the structure of the world but not its intrinsic nature, leaving consciousness — or its precursors — to fill the gap.

Three views

One unfinished argument

Russellian monism descends from a structural argument about physics, sharpens against the hard problem, and bifurcates into two competing answers about what the intrinsic nature of matter must be like. The events below mark its development as a serious metaphysical position.

The map shows how the central thesis — physics is structural — opens space for two answers: panpsychism populates the world with microphenomenal properties; panprotopsychism with protophenomenal ones. Each inherits its own version of the explanatory burden.

Both views accept Russell's structuralism: physics describes how things relate, not what they are. They diverge on what fills the gap. The choice is often presented as a trade — each side accepts a different cost.

Intrinsic property
The categorical bases of physical structure are themselves microphenomenal — tiny, primitive instances of experience.
The categorical bases are protophenomenal — not themselves phenomenal but constitutionally apt to ground phenomenal properties when arranged.
Fundamental ontology
Experience is fundamental and ubiquitous. Electrons have something it is like to be them, however dim.
Experience is not fundamental. The fundamental properties are unknown but suited, by their nature, to give rise to phenomenal properties.
Closes the explanatory gap?
Yes, if combination works: macro-experience is constituted by the right arrangement of micro-experiences.
Promissory. The protophenomenal-to-phenomenal step must do explanatory work that the position itself does not yet specify.
Principal objection
The combination problem in its many forms: subject-summing, palette, structural mismatch, quality combination.
A version of the hard problem reasserts itself: why should non-phenomenal properties, however structured, yield phenomenal ones at all?
Cost
Counterintuitive ontology (experience everywhere) plus the unresolved combination question.
Less ontologically lavish but less explanatory. The "proto-" does heavy lifting it is not yet built to do.
